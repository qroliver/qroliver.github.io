Tourism in the Canary Islands - Part II: Are locals really benefiting?

Posted on 12 December 2025.

As I showed in a previous post, the tourism industry in the Canary Islands has never been stronger than it is today. Visitor numbers have repeadly set new records, average spending per tourist have climbed to unprecedented levels, and the sector is generating more revenue year after year. And it seems that 2025 will be another record-breaking year.

A booming industry with impressive figures, undoubtedly — but how does all this impact the locals? Are Canarians truly benefitting from this bonanza? In this second part, I aim to answer that question by looking at some socioeconomic indicators and seeing how the Canary Islands compare to other Spanish regions.

So, without further ado, let's dive right it!

What's the tourism sector contribution to the regional GDP?

Over the past years, Canarian authorities have repeatedly emphasised the importance of diversifying the archipelago's economy, aiming to reduce dependence on tourism. However, the data suggest that this goal has not been achieved. In 2010, the direct contribution of the tourism sector to the regional GDP was 25%, while by 2024 it had risen to 36.8% (figure 1).

The trend has been steadily upward — only disrupted by the COVID-19 pandemic — making the Canary Islands' ecomomy increasingly reliant on tourism. In fact, 2024 marks an all—time high. Among Spanish regions, the Canary Islands rank second in terms of tourism's direct share of GDP, only slightly behind the Balearic Islands (~40% iin 2023). Notably, these two regions are the only ones in which tourism contributes more than 16% directly to the regional GDP (IMPACTUR data).

How much employment is generated by tourism?

The rising contribution of tourism to the regional GDP has also a clear effect on the job market. In 2024, the tourism sector directly generated 39.4% of all jobs in the Canary Islands, up from 30% in 2010 (figure 2). This steady upward trend reflects the increasing reliance of the local economy on tourism for employment as well, only being disrupted temporarily by the COVID-19 pandemic. In fact, current values are already back to pre-pandemic levels.

Among Spanish regions, only the Balearic Islands (around 40%) have a similar proportion of jobs directly created by tourism, while for other regions this value is below 16% (IMPACTUR data).

Does this increased reliance on a booming tourism sector translate into better wages for locals?

What's the average salary in the Canary Islands?

In the most recent data at the time of writing this post, the average salary in the Canary Islands was the lowest in Spain (figure 3). On average, workers in the archipelago earned around €1000 less than those in Madrid, the region with the highest average salary.

Compared with the Balearic Islands, Canarian workers earned roughly €400 less each month. The same pattern holds for the tertiary sector — which includes the tourist sector (data not shown).

Over the years, the average salary in the Canary Islands has consistently ranked among the lowests in Spain, usually the lowest or second lowest, and remains significantly below the national average (figure 4). Salaries also appear particularly vulnerable to disruptive events. For example, during COVID-19, wages in the Canary Islands dropped more sharply than the national average.

Average salaries grew by 27.81% between 2009 and Q2 2025, while the tourism sector's revenue grew by 101.02% over the same period — a clear mismatch between the workers' earnings and the sector's boom.

When compared with the Balearic Islands, wages in this region have historically been around 13—14% higher than in the Canary Islands — rising to 15—20%+ higher after COVID-19. While both regions experienced similar pandemic-related drops, the Balearics recovered more quickly, with salaries eventually reaching the national average, whereas wages in the Canary Islands remained below this benchmark.

Despite the booming tourism sector and its growing weight in the archipelago's economy, salaries clearly lag behind. But what about the job market? Are there more opportunities for work in the islands?

What's the unemployment rate?

The Canary Islands are among the Spanish regions with the highest unemployment, with a rate well above the national average (14.59 vs. 10.45%; figure 5). When compared with the Balearic Islands — the other region where tourism has a similar dominant role in the economy —, the contrast is striking: the Balearic Islands has the lowest unemployment rate in the country (5.77%), while the Canary Islands' rate is around 2.5 times higher.

The large dependency on tourism also makes the labour market particularly vulnerable to global disruptions. For example, the 2007-8 financial crisis and the COVID-19 pandemic had a greater impact on unemployment in the Canary Islands than in most other regions. For example, between Q4 2007 and Q4 2011, the unemployment rate in the Canary Islands rose from 10.9% to 30.3% — the sharpest increase in Spain during that period (figure 6). For comparison, the national average rose from 8.57% to 22.6%.

While tourism is booming, wages remain low and unemployment is high — suggesting that the living standards of locals might not be as strong as one would expect.

What are the living standard's in the Canary Islands?

Not surprisingly — given the combination of low wages and high unemployment — the Canary Islands rank among the Spanish regions with the highest level of poverty (figure 7). In 2024, 24.6% of the population was at risk of poverty, well above the national average (19.7%) and more than double the rate observed in the Balearic Islands. Particularly striking is that, despite the tourism boom, the share of people at risk of poverty in the Canary Islands is virtually unchanged from twenty years (24.4% in 2004; Eurostat data, not shown).

The archipelago also ranks near the top in severe material and social deprivation, affecting 10.8% of the population — again above both Spain's national average (8.3%) and more than double the Baleric Islands' rate (4.3%; figure 7).

A similar pattern emerges when looking at the percentage of people who cannot afford a protein-rich meal every second day, often used as an indicator of food insecurity. The Canary Islands stand out as the worst-performing region in Spain, with 10.3% of the population affected — 2.34 times the Balearic Islands' rate (4.4%) and well above Spain's national average (6.1%; figure 7).

While both tourism arrivals and revenue have reached record levels, the living standards have not kept pace, and in many indicators the Canary Islands perform among the worst in Spain. This raises the question of how the income distribution is in the archipelago. With low salaries and high poverty despite a booming tourism sector, it seems reasonable to suspect that income inequality might be relatively high — and the data confirm this.

In 2024, the S80/S20 ratio of the Canary Islands was 5.5, meaning that the income of the richest 20% of the population was 5.5 times higher than that of the poorest 20% (figure 8). This places the archipelago as one of the regions with higher income inequality in Spain — with a value similar to Andalusia and Madrid, and significantly above the Balearic Islands (4.4). Across the EU, the Canary Islands rank among the regions with the highest income inequality, with an S80/S20 ratio 2.2 times higher than that of the region with the lowest inequality (Bratislavský kraj, with a ratio of 2.5 in 2024; Eurostat data, not shown).

Wrapping-up

Taken together, the data paints a clear picture. The tourism sector is booming, but locals are not benefitting from it. Despite new records in both arrivals and revenue generated year after year, wages remain among the lowest in Spain — if not the lowest —, unemployment is persistently among the highest, and a significant share of the population faces poverty, material and social deprivation, or even struggles to afford protein-rich meals.

Income inequality is also among the highest in Spain — and in the EU —, suggesting that the wealth generated in the islands is far from being evenly distributed. This points to a scenario in which the economic gains from tourism either are concentrated in few hands or captured by companies and individuals based outside the Canary Islands — or a combination of both.

Media usually highlights record-breaking figures for tourist arrivals and revenue, yet the living conditions of the locals receive less attention. So, when Canarians take to the streets to protest against the current model — demanding change —, the data shows they have good reasons to do so. These protests are not directed at tourists themselves, but at a system that fails to redistribute the wealth generated in the islands.

Another aspect of this economic boom that I haven't covered in the post is how it is shaping the islands' demographics and how it is adding extra pressure on housing. That, however, is for another post. Stay tuned!
