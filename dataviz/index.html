Two centuries of the Monroe Doctrine.

In 1823, James Monroe – the fifth president of the USA – articulated what years later would be known as the Monroe Doctrine. At a time when the independence of Spanish colonies across the Americas was unfolding – by that year, nearly all were independent or close to it – the doctrine declared the Western Hemisphere to be the US sphere of influence, warning that any intervention by European states in the continent would be viewed as a threat to the American security.

Since then, the United States has treated the Americas as its backyard, carrying out over 120 interventions across the continent. From political manipulation and repression to full-scale invasions – such as those in Dominican Republic, Haiti, Grenada, Panama – to backing of military coups and subsequent dictatorships – like Pinochet’s in Chile, overthrowing the democratically elected government of Salvador Allende – or the infamous Operation Condor, in which the US backed right-wing dictatorships across South America to repress political dissidents.

Greenhouse gas emissions vs. EU's 2040 climate target.

11 July 2025.

Last week the EU unveiled its 2040 climate target: a 90% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions (GHG) by 2040 relative to 1990 levels. Are EU countries on track to meet this ambitious goal? Some are, others still have a long way to go.

🇪🇪 🇱🇹 🇱🇻 Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania lead the way.

🇩🇪 Germany, the largest emitter, has nearly halved emissions — the frontrunner among the EU largest economies.

🇪🇸 🇵🇹 🇮🇪 Spain, Portugal, and Ireland are trailing, but their significant renewable potential puts them in a good position to meet the target.

🇨🇾 Cyprus, with higher emission levels than in 1990, faces the largest cut in relative terms.

To meet the target, most countries must reduce GHG emissions by 80-90% compared to today’s levels in just 15 years. That’s a huge challenge, and accelerating the green transition, scaling renewables, and changing habits will be key to achieving it.

Hot days ahead: Adaptation policies in Lanzarote can't wait.

12 May 2025.

Lanzarote is no exception to global climate change, and data from Aemet recorded at Lanzarote Airport confirms this. Record-breaking years like 2023 and 2010 were the hottest on record, closely followed by 2024, which had a mean annual temperature of 22.3°C. This is almost 1°C higher than the 1991-2020 reference period, and around 2°C higher than the 1970s average.

Daily mean temperatures registered at Lanzarote Airport during the period 2020-2024 (last five years) were considerably higher than in the period 1973-1977 (the first five years of the dataset). This trend can be seen for all months of the year, with spring months such as April, May, and June standing out.

Brits are the largest nationality group among tourists visiting the Canary Islands.

25 April 2025.

Historically, the UK has been the largest source of tourists to the Canary Islands. This is something we could see in 2010 as well as in 2024. The UK is also the country that has experienced the largest increase in tourist numbers in absolute terms over the past 15 years (3.11 million, which is 101% more in 2024 compared to 2010).

Germany, historically the second largest market, has seen a more modest increase since 2010, rising from 2.26 millions in 2010 to 2.82 in 2024 (a 24.9% rise). A similar trend can be see in tourists coming from mainland Spain, going from 1.71 million visitors in 2010 to 1.98 in 2024 (+16.1%).

Among the 14 top source countries, France and Italy had the largest increase in relative terms (+348% and +264%, respectively) over the last 15 years. On the other hand, the number of tourists from Nordic countries has remained relatively stable over this period.

What would it take for Denmark to meet NATO's 5% GDP defence target?

Posted on 1 July 2025.

Last week, all NATO countries except Spain agreed to spend 5% of their GDP on defence by 2035. But how much is that in absolute terms?

In the case of Denmark, which currently spends 2.4% of its GDP on defence (2024, NATO estimate), reaching 5% would require an additional 76 billion DKK every year. To put this into perspective, here is how much the Danish government spent in key areas in 2024:

  • Social protection: 574.7 billion DKK.
  • Healthcare: 243.1 billion DKK.
  • Education: 162.5 billion DKK.
  • Defence: 54 billion DKK.
  • Environmental protection: 11.7 billion DKK.

Thus, the extra 76 billion DKK annually would be:

  • Over 6.5 times the current environmental protection expenditure.
  • Nearly half of the education spending.
  • Almost a third of the health budget.
  • 140% more than current government spending on defence.

Spain’s PM was the only one who rejected this target, arguing that spending 5% of GDP on defence is not only unreasonable, but would also severely undermine Europe’s welfare state model.

Numbers shape the narrative, and context helps us understand the consequences. Can Denmark and other allies meet NATO’s goal without compromising core welfare priorities?
