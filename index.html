🌎 Greenland: nothing new in the US backyard – The Monroe Doctrine at play.

In 1823, James Monroe – the fifth president of the USA – articulated what years later would be known as the Monroe Doctrine. At a time when the independence of Spanish colonies across the Americas was unfolding – by that year, nearly all were independent or close to it – the doctrine declared the Western Hemisphere to be the US sphere of influence, warning that any intervention by European states in the continent would be viewed as a threat to the American security.

Since then, the United States has treated the Americas as its backyard, carrying out over 120 interventions across the continent. From political manipulation and repression to full-scale invasions – such as those in Dominican Republic, Haiti, Grenada, Panama – to backing of military coups and subsequent dictatorships – like Pinochet’s in Chile, overthrowing the democratically elected government of Salvador Allende – or the infamous Operation Condor, in which the US backed right-wing dictatorships across South America to repress political dissidents.

The recent abduction of Nicolás Maduro in Venezuela – an event echoing the capture of Manuel Noriega in 1989 – marks the latest in a long line of interventions. With Greenland, Cuba, and Mexico – where the last major US operation resulted in Mexico losing 55% of its territory – as potential future targets in the near future, where will we see the next US move in the continent? A look at history teaches us that the United States’ interventionism knows no bounds – and they don’t hesitate to apply the Machiavellian principle that their end justifies the means.
